Context

A national platform pulling from 30+ government entities, each on its own operational system. Sources were a mix of Oracle and SQL Server, spread across on-prem sites and cloud. I owned the platform end to end.

Challenge

A prior two-year attempt had failed. Most sources had no dependable "last updated" timestamp to drive change detection, data quality varied a lot between entities, and the whole thing had to run daily, stay auditable, and let new entities be added without rebuilding the pipeline each time.

Approach

Since the source timestamps couldn't be trusted, I didn't lean on them. I drove change detection off a primary key plus a row hash, so inserts, updates, and deletes are caught by comparing hashes regardless of what a source does or doesn't report. I split the system into four separate databases so each concern stays isolated and traceable, and I built the loading as a reusable template, so adding a new entity is mostly configuration rather than a fresh custom build.

What I built
  • Four-layer database architecture separating staging, the destination warehouse, rejected records, and monitoring.
  • Hash-based change data capture (primary key plus a row hash) for reliable insert, update, and delete detection without source timestamps.
  • A valid/invalid data quality split where failing rows aren't dropped silently. They're captured with an auto-generated, human-readable reason so they can be triaged and fixed.
  • A reusable load-pattern and stored-procedure template so onboarding a new entity is a repeatable process.
  • Two levels of monitoring and audit logging: job and package execution tracking, plus per-table outcomes (rows inserted, updated, deleted, durations, status).

MoDEE Built on the national platform

Anti-corruption analytics data mart

A denormalized, analysis-ready data mart built on top of the national warehouse for a national anti-corruption body that needed everything joined into wide tables, kept in sync across a hard server boundary.

cross-server incremental loads watermark-driven ETL
Context

The national warehouse is normalized by design. This body needed the opposite for their analysis: every relevant table joined together into wide, denormalized tables they could query directly. The mart also lived on a separate server from the warehouse.

Challenge

Two hard parts. First, the two servers couldn't be linked directly for security reasons, so the truncate-and-reload pattern used on the warehouse wasn't possible here, and the data volume made full reloads impractical anyway. Second, the modeling: building wide tables meant joining across many source tables, which took real understanding of the business behind them, and some of those joins didn't produce a natural primary key.

Approach

Since I couldn't link the servers, I moved data with ETL instead. To avoid reloading everything on every run, I added watermarks on the warehouse side (insert date, update date, business key) so the mart pulls incrementally and only takes what actually changed. On the modeling side, where a clean key didn't fall out of a join, I built logic in CTEs and derived computed columns to construct one and to keep complex relationships resolving correctly rather than collapsing when joined.

What I built
  • Incremental ETL pipelines that move data across the server boundary without a direct link.
  • A watermark scheme on the source side driving the incremental loads.
  • Denormalized wide tables with engineered composite keys and the join logic to populate them correctly.
Stack
SQL ServerSSIST-SQLIncremental ETL
Outcome

The mart stays in sync with the warehouse through incremental loads instead of full reloads, and analysts get correct, queryable wide tables even across complex relationships that would otherwise break on a naive join.

MoDEE Internal

Single source of truth KPI warehouse

A purpose-built KPI mart where each metric is reconciled from several entities and put through layers of business rules, so the ministry has one trusted, traceable place for its KPIs instead of scattered reporting.

multi-entity reconciliation owned end to end (ex. dashboards)
Context

Unlike the national warehouse, this wasn't a broad consolidation. It's targeted: the ministry tracks specific KPIs, so this is a purpose-built mart feeding reporting, shaped around the exact metrics they need.

Challenge

The hard part was business complexity, not volume. A single KPI often isn't sourced from one place. The same subject had to be pulled from multiple entities and reconciled into one view, then put through layer after layer of rules (classification, thresholds, qualification criteria, and more). All of that chained logic could produce just one table, for one page, of one dashboard, with many more behind it.

Approach

A two-stage pattern. Multiple starter queries pull just the slice each KPI needs from each entity and land it in a staging database. Then one larger query joins across those staged tables and applies the business rules to produce the final table for that KPI in the mart. Staging isolates and simplifies each source; the final query handles the reconciliation and logic in one place. Repeat per KPI.

What I built
  • The KPI mart and all the ETL behind it: the starter queries, the staging layer, and the consolidating queries.
  • Multi-entity reconciliation per subject, with the business rules encoded as the transformation layer.
  • Owned everything end to end except the dashboards, which read from finished, ready tables.
Stack
SQL ServerT-SQLETLPower BI
Outcome

Trustworthy, consistent KPI definitions in one place, each traceable back through its sources and rules, replacing scattered and inconsistent reporting.

Pure Analytica Airport International Group Data engineering

360 balanced scorecard data mart

Pulled a company's KPIs out of scattered manual Excel files into one consolidated balanced scorecard, driven by a config table so KPIs can change without reworking the pipeline.

manual Excel → one scorecard config-driven ETL
Context

Airport International Group had no real way to get one overall read on enterprise performance. The KPIs existed, but they were tracked manually across scattered Excel files, with different teams each reporting their own piece. They needed one consolidated balanced scorecard.

Challenge

Beyond consolidating a mess of manual sources, the system had to stay maintainable. A scorecard's KPIs change, and you don't want a design where every change means reworking the ETL.

Approach

A config-driven design. A central config table holds each KPI as a row: its ID, its name, the function it targets, and the calculation query itself stored in the table. The SSIS ETL then runs a dynamic query that reads each KPI's stored query, executes it, and writes the result into its destination table. So adding or changing a KPI becomes a config change, not an ETL change.

What I built
  • Implemented the KPI queries and mainly built the dynamic SSIS ETL that reads and runs them from config.
  • Worked with the team on the config table holding the KPI calculations.
  • The consolidated scorecard data mart the reporting sits on.
Stack
SQL ServerSSIST-SQLDynamic ETL
Outcome

Every manually tracked KPI pulled into one consolidated scorecard instead of scattered Excel files and team-by-team reporting, with a design where KPIs are added or changed through config rather than a pipeline rebuild.

SAP integrations & supplier self-service portal

PO creation → suppliers static → dynamic queries
Context

As SAP Solutions & Integration Specialist I ran the SAP system and built the integrations and reporting around it. The supplier portal was the centerpiece: a place for suppliers to raise their own purchase orders as drafts, leaving the internal team to review and approve rather than create every order by hand.

Challenge

The existing portal implementation had real problems. The queries behind it were static, so anything new added to the system, a new warehouse or a new item, simply didn't show up. Some queries were slow. And there were structural issues from parts having been built the wrong way.

Approach

I reworked the static queries to be dynamic, so the portal reflects new things automatically instead of needing a manual edit every time the system changes. I optimized the slow ones, and on the broader structural issues I consulted on the fix and implemented part of it. Alongside the portal, I integrated external systems into SAP and built the reporting layer.

What I built
  • The supplier self-service flow: suppliers create their own draft purchase orders, the team only approves.
  • Dynamic, self-updating queries behind the portal that pick up new system items automatically.
  • Performance fixes on the slow queries, and part of the fix for the structural issues.
  • External system integrations into SAP, including an online store, plus the reporting layer and its queries.
